## Query 2: Deep Details & Standards

**Charles Dow (1851–1902)** is widely regarded as the father of technical analysis for developing **Dow Theory**, which identifies market trends and price patterns through analysis of stock market data published in *The Wall Street Journal*. He also examined the relationship between volume and price, laying groundwork for chart-based strategies, though he did not advocate it as a trading system.[1][2][3]

**Joseph de la Vega** published *Confusion de Confusiones* in **1688**, one of the earliest works documenting Dutch financial markets, speculative techniques, and methods to predict stock price movements based on observed patterns.[1][2]

**Homma Munehisa** (early 18th century, Japan) pioneered candlestick charting techniques by studying historical rice market data, which evolved into a core tool for visualizing price action and forecasting trends in technical analysis.[2]

**Richard W. Schabacker** (1920s–1930s) advanced Dow's ideas in books like *Stock Market Theory and Practice* and *Technical Market Analysis*, systematizing chart patterns and trend analysis.[2]

**Robert D. Edwards and John Magee** published *Technical Analysis of Stock Trends* in **1948**, a seminal work focusing exclusively on trend analysis and chart patterns, still in use today and considered foundational to the discipline.[2]

**Ralph Nelson Elliott** developed the **Elliott Wave Principle** in the 1930s, describing stock market movements as repetitive wave patterns driven by crowd psychology, highly descriptive of historical market data from 1857 onward.[3][7]

**W.D. Gann** (early 20th century) integrated mathematics, geometry, and astrological concepts into predictive tools like Gann angles for forecasting price and time movements with noted accuracy.[3]

**Jesse Livermore** (early 20th century) used ticker tape for real-time price tracking and volume estimation via "market testing" to identify market phases like trends and corrections, as detailed in his 1940s book.[1][2]

Later pioneers include **Edson Gould** (1930s), who predicted Dow Jones movements using indicators like the Senti-Meter[1]; **Andrew Lo and Craig McKinlay** (late 1980s–2000), whose studies (e.g., 1962–1996 data analysis) found technical indicators provided predictive value, challenging random walk hypotheses[2]; and **Tushar Chande** (1989), co-creator of the Aroon Indicator for trend strength.[1] The **CMT Association**, founded in **1967**, formalized the field with the Chartered Market Technician credential.[1]
